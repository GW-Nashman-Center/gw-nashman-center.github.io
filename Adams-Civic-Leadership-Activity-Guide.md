# Civic Leadership Self-Assessment & Public Impact Map

**Creator**: Corey D. Adams, Ed.D., Public Sector Chief Learning Officer and Adjunct Professor, Human and Organizational Learning, Graduate School of Education and Human Development, George Washington University – ![coreyadams@gwu.edu](mailto:coreyadams@gwu.edu)

**Original Class Context**: Adaptable across disciplines; not tied to a specific course

**Purpose**: To support student reflection on civic identity and leadership using a civic-based leadership framework.

**Activity Type**: Leadership self-assessment, civic identity reflection, and peer dialogue

**Time**: Flexible; can be completed in 1–2 class sessions or partially outside of class

**Class Size**: Adaptable for small seminars or large lecture-based courses

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this activity, students will be able to:

- Identify and self-assess their civic leadership tendencies across four core domains: public service motivation, democratic accountability, political astuteness, and public value orientation.
- Reflect on their lived experiences and values as they relate to civic engagement and public contribution.
- Synthesize their insights into a visual or narrative artifact (Public Impact Map) representing their personal civic identity.
- Engage in peer dialogue to explore diverse perspectives on civic leadership and community contribution.


# Pre-Activity Preparation

## Student: Required Prerequisite Knowledge/Skills

- Complete the Civic Leadership Self-Assessment and review personal results. Students should bring their results to class for discussion and reflection.

## Instructor: Required Materials & Set up

- Review the Civic Leadership Self-Assessment instructions and ensure students have access in advance.
- Prepare to facilitate small-group or paired peer dialogues.
- No specialized technology is required beyond discussion tools and optional slides.

## Accessibility Considerations

- Ensure materials are available in accessible formats.
- Allow flexibility in how students complete and present the Public Impact Map (written narrative, infographic, or spoken presentation).

# Activity Instructions

Blended Format:** Pre-class reflection + in-class dialogue + optional follow-up

| Time                         | Instructor Delivery                                                                 | Student Actions                                                                                 |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Before Class                | Distribute the Civic Leadership Self-Assessment. Review instructions and clarify expectations. | Complete the self-assessment and reflect on individual scores and insights.                     |
| 10 min                      | Introduction & Engagement: Provide overview of civic leadership and relevance.        | Participate in discussion on civic identity.                                                    |
| 25 min                      | Phase 1 – Small Group Dialogue: Facilitate conversations using guiding prompts.       | Share and compare self-assessment results; explore civic perspectives.                          |
| 20 min                      | Phase 2 – Public Impact Map Planning: Explain and support students in map development. | Begin outlining or planning their map (written or visual).                                      |
| Optional Homework or Class 2 | Host a gallery or follow-up dialogue for sharing maps.                                 | Finalize and present or submit Public Impact Map.                                               |



Sample Peer Dialogue Prompts:
- Which of the four civic leadership domains resonated most with you—and why?
- Were there any areas where you rated yourself lower? What experiences might help you grow in that area?
- How do your academic interests or personal background shape your civic motivations or leadership values?
- What’s one way you envision contributing to public good—either now or in the future?



# Assessment & Evaluation

Formative Assessment
- Monitor participation during peer dialogue.
- Optional feedback on Public Impact Map to affirm effort and clarity.

Summative Assessment (Optional)
- Completion credit or inclusion in broader reflections. No formal grading required.


# Additional Notes for the Instructor
- Adaptable across disciplines: Instructors can connect the civic leadership framework to relevant themes in their field.
- Encourage students to reflect on public service both within and beyond political systems.
- Flexibility in map format supports creative and inclusive participation.
